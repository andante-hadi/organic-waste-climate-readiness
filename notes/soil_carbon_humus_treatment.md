# Soil Carbon And Humus Treatment

Date: 2026-05-23

## Purpose

This note documents how soil carbon and humus effects are treated in the current manuscript draft. The central climate screen does not credit compost or digestate soil-carbon effects. This is a limitation, but it is preferable to applying a poorly constrained global credit.

## Current Treatment

Central pathway results include:

- avoided disposal methane;
- process emissions;
- electricity substitution for AD-electricity;
- transport-fuel substitution for AD-bio-CNG;
- upstream food-system avoidance for prevention.

Central pathway results do not include:

- soil organic carbon accrual from compost or digestate;
- humus formation;
- long-term soil fertility effects;
- avoided irrigation or erosion effects;
- crop-yield effects;
- region-specific nutrient availability and losses.

## Why Excluded From The Central Screen

Soil-carbon and humus benefits are highly context-specific. They depend on:

- compost or digestate quality;
- application rate;
- crop and soil type;
- baseline soil organic carbon;
- climate;
- tillage and land-management practices;
- time horizon and permanence;
- substitution of other organic amendments;
- whether carbon storage is additional.

Using a single global soil-carbon credit would risk overstating composting benefits in some regions and understating them in others. For a global country-level screen, it is safer to acknowledge this missing value explicitly and keep soil carbon in the limitation/future-work layer unless a defensible spatial soil model is added.

## ecoinvent-Derived Humus Context

The ecoinvent documentation copied for composting and anaerobic digestion reports humus-related factors and notes that compost and digestate have different capacities to build humus. The copied tables indicate that mature compost has higher humus-C potential than fresh compost, solid digestate and liquid digestate. These values support the qualitative claim that composting and digestate may provide soil-carbon benefits not captured by the central GWP100 screen.

## Recommended Manuscript Wording

Use this wording in Discussion:

> Composting remains beneficial but is undervalued in the central climate-only screen because soil organic carbon, humus formation, nutrient cycling, agronomic quality, cost and deployability are not fully monetized or converted to CO2-equivalent credits. We do not apply a single global soil-carbon credit because soil responses depend on climate, soil type, crop system, baseline organic carbon, application practice and permanence. The composting result should therefore be interpreted as a conservative climate-only estimate rather than as a full assessment of composting's agronomic and circular-economy value.

## Future Sensitivity Option

A future sensitivity could apply low/central/high soil-carbon retention factors for compost and digestate, but only with clear caveats. A better approach would link compost/digestate application to spatial soil, climate and cropland data.

## Reviewer Response Logic

If reviewers ask why composting is lower:

1. Explain that the main model is GWP100-focused and avoids uncertain global soil-carbon credits.
2. Point to the nutrient-credit sensitivity, which modestly increases composting and AD.
3. State that composting has important non-climate and soil benefits that are not fully represented.
4. Offer an additional soil-carbon sensitivity in revision if requested.
