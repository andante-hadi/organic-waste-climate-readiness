# Results Draft: Nature Sustainability Version

## Organic Municipal Waste Is A Large Methane-Relevant Flow

The What a Waste 3.0 country dataset indicates that 217 countries and economies generated approximately 1.064 billion tonnes of food and green organic municipal solid waste in 2022. Of this amount, 516 million tonnes were associated with landfill, open dumping, uncollected waste, or unaccounted treatment flows in countries where treatment-share data were available.

Using first-pass IPCC-style methane screening factors, this unmanaged/disposal fraction corresponds to 18.9 Mt CH4 per year, or 514.1 Mt CO2e per year using a 100-year methane GWP. These values should be interpreted as a prioritization screen rather than a final national inventory. The purpose of this first layer is to identify where organic municipal waste creates a large near-term climate opportunity before comparing intervention pathways.

## Four Pathways Produce Different Global And Spatial Priorities

We compared four intervention pathways: prevention, composting, anaerobic digestion with electricity recovery, and anaerobic digestion with biomethane/bio-CNG production. At the global level, the deterministic screen estimates net GWP100 benefits of 248.9 Mt CO2e per year for AD-bio-CNG, 232.8 Mt CO2e per year for prevention, 229.6 Mt CO2e per year for AD-electricity, and 193.5 Mt CO2e per year for composting.

The deterministic global ranking is not a universal technology ranking. It reflects the assumed intervention rates, methane avoidance, process emissions, country-specific grid substitution, and transport-fuel displacement. AD-bio-CNG ranks highest globally because it combines avoided disposal methane with transport-fuel substitution. Prevention remains close to the highest global estimate because it avoids both upstream food-system emissions and downstream waste burdens. Composting is consistently climate-positive but lower in this GWP100-only screen because nutrient recovery, soil carbon, cost, simplicity, and institutional deployability are only partially represented.

Country-level rankings reveal the policy relevance of spatial heterogeneity. Among countries and economies with sufficient pathway data, prevention is the leading deterministic pathway in 98 cases, AD-bio-CNG in 50 cases, and AD-electricity in 4 cases; 65 countries and economies lack sufficient data for pathway assignment. This distribution shows why the waste hierarchy cannot be converted into a single climate-investment rule without considering national baselines and substitution contexts.

## Uncertainty Changes The Interpretation

Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis gives a more cautious interpretation than the deterministic ranking. At the global level, median net GWP100 benefits are 244.4 Mt CO2e per year for prevention, 242.3 Mt CO2e per year for AD-bio-CNG, 230.4 Mt CO2e per year for AD-electricity, and 190.7 Mt CO2e per year for composting. The 5-95% ranges are widest for prevention because both achievable prevention rates and avoided upstream food-production burdens vary substantially. AD-bio-CNG remains high-potential but is sensitive to biogas yield, methane recovery, upgrading/compression electricity, and displaced transport fuel.

The country-level robustness results strengthen the central hierarchy-readiness argument. Prevention is the robust winner in 107 countries/economies, AD-bio-CNG in 31, and AD-electricity in 5. Nine countries have no robust winner, and 65 remain missing or insufficiently characterized. Thus, prevention is the most robust pathway across countries even though AD-bio-CNG is the largest deterministic global opportunity in the central parameterization.

## Readiness Separates Immediate Action From Strategic Build-Out

The readiness screen separates mitigation potential from implementation plausibility. Countries are classified into four decision spaces: immediate priority, strategic build-out, no-regret/complementary, and longer-term/local fit. The first readiness screen identifies 43 countries/economies as immediate priorities, 27 as strategic build-out cases, 64 as no-regret or complementary action contexts, 18 as longer-term/local-fit contexts, and 65 as missing or insufficiently characterized.

This layer changes how pathway results should be interpreted. A high-potential country with stronger collection coverage, urbanization, income, data completeness, and existing biological treatment signals is a plausible near-term investment candidate. A high-potential country with lower readiness may still be important, but the policy problem shifts from immediate deployment to source separation, collection, institutional capacity, offtake markets, and infrastructure build-out. Lower-potential but higher-readiness countries remain useful no-regret contexts, especially for composting, prevention, and incremental biological treatment expansion.

## Reinterpreting The Waste Hierarchy

The results do not imply that energy recovery should displace prevention as the first principle of organic waste policy. Rather, they show that the waste hierarchy and climate-investment prioritization answer different questions. The hierarchy states a preferred order of action; the country-level screen asks where marginal mitigation can be delivered under existing waste baselines, energy systems, uncertainty, and readiness constraints.

For Nature Sustainability, the central result is therefore not that one pathway "wins". The central result is that organic municipal waste transitions require a two-level decision logic: prevention should remain protected as the upstream priority, while unavoidable or already-generated organic waste should be routed through country-specific treatment and recovery pathways shaped by methane burden, energy substitution, soil and nutrient value, and implementation readiness.
