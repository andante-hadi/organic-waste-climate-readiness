# Cover Letter Draft: Nature Sustainability Analysis

Dear Editors,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "Operationalizing the waste hierarchy for climate-ready organic waste transitions," for consideration as an Analysis in *Nature Sustainability*.

Organic municipal waste is a major source of avoidable methane, but countries lack a global decision framework for translating the waste hierarchy into climate investment priorities. Prevention is rightly placed at the top of the waste hierarchy, especially for food waste, yet countries also face large quantities of unavoidable or already-generated organic waste that are landfilled, dumped, uncollected or otherwise unmanaged. In these contexts, climate-prioritized action depends not only on hierarchy principles, but also on methane baselines, energy substitution, uncertainty and implementation readiness.

Our manuscript develops a global country-level decision framework for food and green organic municipal waste across 217 countries and economies. We combine World Bank What a Waste 3.0 data, World Development Indicators, Ember electricity carbon intensity, ecoinvent-derived process parameters and Natural Earth boundaries to compare four pathways: prevention, composting, anaerobic digestion with electricity recovery, and anaerobic digestion with biomethane/bio-CNG production. We then evaluate pathway rankings through Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis and a system-readiness typology that converts hierarchy principles into a policy-sequencing tool.

The analysis estimates 1.064 billion tonnes per year of food and green organic municipal waste in 2022, including 516 million tonnes associated with landfill, dumping, uncollected waste or unaccounted treatment flows in countries with treatment data. This corresponds to a first-pass methane burden of 514 Mt CO2e per year. Prevention provides the largest global GWP100 benefit and is the most robust country-level winner, but AD-bio-CNG remains a spatially specific recovery opportunity where methane avoidance, fuel substitution and infrastructure readiness align. The readiness layer separates countries into immediate priorities, strategic build-out cases, no-regret or complementary contexts, and longer-term local-fit opportunities.

We believe the manuscript fits *Nature Sustainability* because it provides a cross-disciplinary framework for operationalizing the waste hierarchy under real-world sustainability constraints. The study connects climate mitigation, circular economy, food-waste prevention, resource recovery, energy substitution, urban infrastructure, uneven development and policy prioritization. Its central contribution is not a universal technology ranking, but a spatial decision logic: prevention should remain protected as the upstream priority, while unavoidable or already-generated organic waste should be routed through context-specific treatment and recovery pathways shaped by methane burden, energy systems and readiness.

The manuscript is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and all authors have approved its submission. The authors have no related manuscripts under consideration or in press elsewhere, and we have had no prior discussions with a *Nature Sustainability* editor about this work. Public input data, processed outputs and analysis scripts are available at https://github.com/andante-hadi/organic-waste-climate-readiness. Licensed ecoinvent source files and exchange-level inventory data will not be redistributed; derived parameters and process choices are documented transparently in the manuscript and Supplementary Information in accordance with licence constraints. Process-emission, biogas-yield and biogas net-calorific-value parameters are derived from ecoinvent 3.12 Cutoff unit-process exports and documentation; methane-equivalent share is treated through uncertainty and stress tests.
