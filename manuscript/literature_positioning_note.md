# Literature Positioning Note

This note defines the manuscript's literature gap for a Nature Sustainability submission. It should guide the Introduction, cover letter and reviewer response strategy.

## Existing Literature Clusters

### Global food-system and food-waste emissions

Recent food-system work establishes that food systems are a very large share of anthropogenic greenhouse-gas emissions and that food loss and waste carries major environmental burdens across the supply chain [17-21]. This literature is essential for the Introduction because it makes food waste a climate-relevant topic, not only a sanitation or resource-efficiency topic.

However, most global food loss and waste studies focus on upstream production, supply-chain losses, nutritional losses or aggregate food-system emissions. They do not provide a country-level decision framework for downstream municipal treatment pathways for the food and green fraction of municipal solid waste.

### Waste hierarchy and circular economy

The waste hierarchy is well established in policy and academic literature, and for food waste it has been formalized as a framework in which prevention and redistribution sit above recycling, recovery and disposal [15,25,26,32]. This literature supports the manuscript's stance that prevention should remain the first principle.

The gap is operational. The hierarchy does not say which countries should prioritize prevention programs, composting capacity, anaerobic digestion with electricity recovery, or biomethane/bio-CNG infrastructure when large amounts of organic waste are already generated, source separation is incomplete, methane baselines differ and implementation capacity varies.

### Technology-specific LCA and biowaste treatment

Comparative LCA studies and reviews show that composting, anaerobic digestion, landfill, incineration and emerging food-waste valorization pathways can have different environmental outcomes depending on system boundaries, avoided products, energy substitution, feedstock quality and local assumptions [22-24,28,33]. This literature is useful for defending the pathway structure and uncertainty analysis.

The limitation is scale and decision context. Many studies are facility-, city- or region-specific, while reviews synthesize technologies without producing a global country-level prioritization screen that joins waste generation, current disposal practice, electricity carbon intensity, bio-CNG substitution, uncertainty and readiness.

### Compost, digestate and soil-carbon value

Compost and digestate literature shows that biological treatment should not be evaluated only as methane avoidance. Compost can contribute soil organic matter and potential greenhouse-gas benefits, while digestate can recycle nutrients and affect crop growth [34-37]. This supports treating composting as a conservative result in the central climate-only screen, because soil and nutrient benefits are context-dependent and are not fully captured by a uniform global credit.

The manuscript should explicitly state that low composting ranking in the GWP100 central screen is not a judgement against composting. It reflects conservative accounting, incomplete soil/nutrient valuation and a focus on country-level climate prioritization rather than agronomic optimization.

### Methane mitigation and system readiness

Methane-focused assessments highlight the near-term importance of cutting methane from waste systems [16,31]. Global solid-waste reviews also stress that implementation depends on governance, collection systems, stakeholder participation, finance, technologies and data quality [26].

The manuscript's originality is to connect methane opportunity with readiness. A country with large unmanaged organic waste is not automatically an immediate investment priority if collection, source separation, biological treatment capacity, offtake markets or data systems are weak. Conversely, a smaller opportunity with stronger readiness may be a realistic near-term deployment case.

## Claim Of Novelty

The study's publishable contribution is not that one pathway is universally better than another. The contribution is a global climate-readiness framework for operationalizing the waste hierarchy under uneven country conditions.

The framework brings together five elements that are usually separated:

1. food and green organic municipal waste generation and disposal baselines;
2. first-pass methane burden from landfilling, dumping, uncollected and unaccounted flows;
3. four pathway options: prevention, composting, AD-electricity and AD-bio-CNG;
4. uncertainty in pathway parameters and country-level robust winners;
5. readiness classes that distinguish immediate priorities from strategic build-out contexts.

## Best Nature Sustainability Framing

The strongest framing is:

> The waste hierarchy remains right, but it is not enough to allocate climate investment across countries. Organic municipal waste transitions need a spatial decision logic that protects prevention while identifying where methane avoidance, biological treatment, bioenergy recovery and system readiness align.

Avoid framing the paper as:

- AD versus prevention.
- Bio-CNG is globally best.
- The waste hierarchy is wrong.
- Composting is weak.

Use instead:

- prevention is the most robust country-level principle;
- bio-CNG is high-potential but assumption- and readiness-sensitive;
- composting is conservative in the climate-only screen and may have broader soil/nutrient value;
- readiness prevents theoretical mitigation from being mistaken for deployable mitigation.
