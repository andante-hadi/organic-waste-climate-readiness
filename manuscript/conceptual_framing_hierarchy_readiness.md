# Conceptual Framing: Waste Hierarchy, Climate Priority, and Readiness

This study does not reject the waste hierarchy. Instead, it tests how the hierarchy translates into country-level climate priorities for the organic fraction of municipal solid waste (OFMSW).

The hierarchy remains normatively important: prevention avoids upstream food production burdens and downstream waste-management burdens before they occur. However, once large OFMSW flows already exist and are currently landfilled, dumped, uncollected, or unaccounted, the marginal climate benefit of intervention depends on national context. Countries differ in unmanaged organic waste mass, landfill/dump methane risk, grid carbon intensity, transport-fuel substitution value, collection coverage, urbanization, income, existing biological treatment, and data completeness.

The study therefore separates three concepts that are often conflated:

1. **Hierarchy preference**: prevention is upstream and should be protected as the first principle.
2. **Marginal climate opportunity**: AD-electricity, AD-bio-CNG, composting, or prevention may deliver different near-term GWP100 benefits depending on baseline methane and displaced energy or fuel.
3. **System readiness**: a high-mitigation pathway may still require strategic build-out if source separation, biological treatment capacity, fuel standards, vehicle fleets, offtake markets, or institutional capacity are weak.

This framing avoids a false conclusion that energy recovery "beats" prevention. A better interpretation is that the waste hierarchy gives direction, while spatial climate-readiness analysis helps sequence investment.

## Four Decision Classes

The current readiness layer classifies countries into four decision spaces:

- **Immediate priority**: high mitigation potential and high readiness.
- **Strategic build-out**: high mitigation potential but lower readiness.
- **No-regret / complementary**: lower mitigation potential but higher readiness.
- **Longer-term / local fit**: lower mitigation potential and lower readiness.

In the first readiness screen, 43 countries/economies fall into the immediate-priority class, 27 into strategic build-out, 64 into no-regret/complementary action, 18 into longer-term/local fit, and 65 remain missing or insufficiently characterized.

## Robustness Interpretation

The four-pathway deterministic screen finds the following global net GWP100 benefits:

- AD-bio-CNG: 248.9 Mt CO2e/y.
- Prevention: 232.8 Mt CO2e/y.
- AD-electricity: 229.6 Mt CO2e/y.
- Composting: 193.5 Mt CO2e/y.

The Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis shows a more nuanced result:

- Prevention has the largest median global benefit and is the most robust country-level winner.
- AD-bio-CNG has high global potential but is more sensitive to assumptions about biogas yield, upgrading electricity, methane recovery, and displaced transport fuel.
- AD-electricity is robust in a small number of countries where grid substitution and methane avoidance are favorable.
- Composting remains beneficial but is not the dominant climate pathway in the current GWP100-only screen; its role may be stronger when soil carbon, nutrient recovery, low-cost deployability, and non-climate benefits are included.

## Suggested High-Impact Claim

The waste hierarchy is necessary but insufficient for climate-prioritizing OFMSW transitions. A country-specific strategy should preserve prevention as a first principle while using methane baselines, energy substitution, and readiness constraints to identify where biological treatment, bio-CNG, and composting can deliver near-term climate gains.
