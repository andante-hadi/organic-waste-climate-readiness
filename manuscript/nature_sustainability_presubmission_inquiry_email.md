**Proposed title:** Reconciling the waste hierarchy with climate-readiness priorities for global organic municipal waste

Organic municipal waste is usually governed through the waste hierarchy, which rightly prioritizes prevention. However, countries also face large flows of already-generated or unavoidable food and green organic municipal waste that are landfilled, dumped, uncollected or otherwise poorly managed. For climate policy, the unresolved question is not whether prevention should remain the first principle, but how countries should sequence prevention, methane avoidance, biological treatment and resource recovery when methane baselines, energy systems and implementation readiness differ.

We address this question by developing a global country-level climate-readiness screening framework for 217 countries and economies. The analysis combines World Bank What a Waste 3.0 data, World Development Indicators, Ember electricity carbon intensity, ecoinvent-derived process parameters and Natural Earth boundaries. We compare four pathways for food and green organic municipal waste: prevention, composting, anaerobic digestion with electricity recovery and anaerobic digestion with biomethane/bio-CNG production. We then evaluate pathway rankings under Monte Carlo uncertainty and classify countries using a system-readiness typology.

The analysis estimates 1.064 billion tonnes per year of food and green organic municipal waste in 2022. Of this, 516 million tonnes were landfilled, dumped, uncollected or unaccounted in countries with treatment data, corresponding to a first-pass methane burden of approximately 514 Mt CO2e per year. In the deterministic GWP100 screen, AD-bio-CNG provides the largest global benefit, but uncertainty analysis identifies prevention as the most robust country-level winner. The readiness layer separates 43 immediate-priority countries/economies from 27 strategic build-out cases, 64 no-regret or complementary contexts and 18 longer-term/local-fit contexts; 65 countries/economies are classified as missing or insufficiently characterized, with missingness concentrated in composition, treatment and collection data.

The central contribution is a spatial decision framework for operationalizing the waste hierarchy under real-world sustainability constraints. The results do not argue that energy recovery should replace prevention. Rather, they show that hierarchy principles, marginal climate opportunity, uncertainty and implementation readiness need to be evaluated together when prioritizing organic municipal waste transitions.

We believe the manuscript fits *Nature Sustainability* because it connects climate mitigation, methane reduction, circular economy, food-waste prevention, waste infrastructure, energy substitution, data capacity and policy sequencing across countries. The work is intended as a screening and prioritization framework, not as a facility-level LCA or a final national greenhouse-gas inventory.

The analysis uses public datasets and licence-compliant ecoinvent-derived parameters. Process-emission and nutrient parameters are derived from available ecoinvent 3.12 documentation; biogas yield currently uses a transparent ecoinvent 3.1 cutoff proxy because 3.12 exchange-level access is being pursued. The manuscript includes uncertainty analysis and stress tests around the parameters most relevant to AD-bio-CNG, as well as GWP20, landfill-gas-capture, nutrient-credit, city-layer and missing-data sensitivity/diagnostic analyses. Public input data, processed outputs and code will be deposited in a public repository; proprietary ecoinvent source files will not be redistributed.

We would be grateful for your advice on whether this manuscript is within the journal's scope before we proceed to full submission.
