# Introduction Draft

Food loss and waste is one of the largest underused climate-mitigation opportunities in the global food system. Recent assessments estimate that food loss and waste contributes about 8-10% of global greenhouse-gas emissions, through the land, energy, water, fertilizer, processing, transport, and retail systems used to produce food that is ultimately not consumed. The climate relevance of food waste does not end at the point of discard. Once food and other biodegradable organic materials enter municipal waste systems, they can generate methane if landfilled, dumped, left uncollected, or otherwise managed under anaerobic conditions. This downstream methane burden makes organic municipal waste a near-term climate issue as well as a food-system and sanitation issue.

The waste hierarchy provides a clear normative starting point: prevention should be prioritized before reuse, recycling, recovery, or disposal. For food waste, this principle is especially compelling because prevention avoids both upstream food-system emissions and downstream waste-management burdens. Yet the hierarchy does not by itself resolve the investment choices faced by countries and cities where large organic waste flows already exist, source separation is incomplete, disposal methane is substantial, and energy or fuel systems differ widely. In these contexts, prevention remains essential, but unavoidable or already-generated organic municipal waste still requires treatment pathways that minimize climate impacts and fit local infrastructure.

Organic municipal solid waste pathways differ in the climate mechanisms they activate. Composting can avoid landfill methane and recover organic matter and nutrients, but its climate balance depends on process emissions, compost quality, soil effects, and avoided fertilizer production. Anaerobic digestion can avoid disposal methane while producing biogas, but its value depends on whether the biogas substitutes grid electricity, heat, fossil gas, or transport fuels, and on methane leakage and digestate management. Upgrading biogas to biomethane or bio-compressed natural gas can connect organic waste management with cleaner transport-fuel systems, but only where fuel standards, compression, storage, distribution, vehicle demand, and institutional capacity are plausible. Prevention differs from all treatment pathways because it acts upstream, but its achievable rate and avoided production burden vary by food system, income, supply chain, and behavior.

Most evidence on organic waste treatment is not designed to answer this country-level prioritization problem. Technology-specific life-cycle assessments often evaluate individual facilities, cities, or feedstocks under local assumptions. Global food-waste studies quantify the scale of food loss and waste, but generally do not compare downstream municipal treatment alternatives across countries. Waste-management datasets describe generation, composition, and treatment shares, but are rarely connected to energy substitution, pathway-specific mitigation, uncertainty, and implementation readiness in a single global framework. As a result, policy discussions can become polarized between a universal reading of the waste hierarchy and technology-specific claims that anaerobic digestion, composting, biomethane, or bio-CNG is inherently preferable.

Here we develop a country-level climate-readiness screening framework for the food and green organic fraction of municipal solid waste across 217 countries and economies. We combine World Bank What a Waste 3.0 data on waste generation, composition, collection, treatment, and projections with World Development Indicators, Ember electricity carbon intensity, ecoinvent-derived process parameters, and Natural Earth country boundaries. We compare four pathways: food-waste prevention, composting, anaerobic digestion with electricity recovery, and anaerobic digestion with biomethane/bio-CNG production. We then add two decision layers: Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis to identify robust pathway winners, and a system-readiness index that distinguishes immediate priorities from strategic build-out cases.

Our aim is not to overturn the waste hierarchy. Instead, we ask how the hierarchy should be operationalized when countries differ in methane baselines, energy systems, treatment capacity, data quality, and readiness. This approach separates three questions that are often conflated: which pathway is preferred in principle, which pathway offers the largest marginal climate benefit under current national conditions, and which pathway is feasible enough to justify near-term investment. By reconciling hierarchy, climate opportunity, uncertainty, and readiness, the study provides a spatial decision framework for prioritizing organic municipal waste transitions.
